Family, Acceptance, and Identity: The Everyday Reality of Being Queer

Rittija Banik

For many of us, “family” is supposed to mean safety, a place where we can exist without fear or explanation. But for countless individuals in the LGBTQ+ community, home is often the first place where they learn that being themselves might not be enough.

One of the most important things that still needs to be said, again and again, is this: being a part of the LGBTQ+ community is not a choice. It is not a phase, not a rebellion, not something picked up from the internet or influenced by others. No amount of medical treatment can change their sexuality. It is simply a part of who someone is. Just like any other identity, it exists naturally. The struggle, therefore, is not about “becoming” queer, it is about surviving in a world that refuses to accept it easily.

For many, the process of identifying oneself comes earlier than acceptance. A person may know who they are, may understand their feelings, but still hesitate to express it. Why? Because acceptance, especially from family, takes time, and sometimes, it never fully comes. This gap between self-realisation and social acceptance creates a quiet but constant tension in everyday life.

This tension shows up in small, ordinary moments. It is in the hesitation before answering questions like “When will you get married?” It is in the discomfort when relatives make casual jokes about gender roles. It is in the need to change pronouns while talking about someone you love. These are not dramatic, one-time struggles but are daily negotiations with identity.

Bullying, too, is not limited to schools or public spaces. It often exists within homes in subtle forms like mockery, dismissal, or simply silence. A child may not be thrown out of the house, but they may be made to feel invisible. And that invisibility can be just as damaging. It creates self-doubt, loneliness, and the feeling that love must be earned by changing who you are.

In many cases, transgender individuals are not just made to feel invisible but are actually forced to leave or are driven out of their homes. With no support system, many are left with very limited options for survival. Some are pushed into begging, and later, some may even end up in sex work, not by choice but because society has denied them basic opportunities for education, employment, and dignity.

And yet, there are moments of resistance and hope. A recent incident from the Sundarbans, where two women, Riya and Rakhi, chose to marry each other despite social pressure, reminds us that love continues to exist even in the face of rejection. What stood out was not just their courage, but also the support they received from parts of their community. It showed that acceptance, even if not universal, is possible and growing.

For many queer individuals, when biological families fail to provide support, they find or create “chosen families.” These are friends, partners, and communities who offer what should have been given unconditionally such as understanding, respect, and love. These relationships prove that family is not always about blood, but about acceptance.

At the same time, change is slowly unfolding. Conversations around identity are becoming more visible. Families are beginning to listen, even if hesitantly. Acceptance may not happen overnight, but even small shifts matter. Because at the end of the day, acceptance is not about grand gestures. It is about everyday actions, using the right name, respecting someone’s truth, and allowing them to exist without fear. Identity does not demand approval. It only asks for space.

And perhaps that is where real change begins, not in theory, not in slogans, but in the quiet, everyday decision to choose love over judgment.
